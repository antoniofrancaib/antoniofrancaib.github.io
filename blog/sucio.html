Some people are concerned that the advancement of Artificial Intelligence may lead to an existential catastrophe, that is, the destruction of humanity’s long-term potential. The following is a brief distillation of Open Philanthropy Senior Research Analyst Joseph Carlsmith’s "Is power-seeking AI an existential risk?" paper, where a concrete AI-related catastrophic scenario is depicted. This scenario includes a given set of hypothetical events and the final aim is to assign a probability of the occurrence of these hypotheses by 2070—just to mean within our lifetimes.

The conceptual use of “power” here means something like the type of thing that helps a wide variety of agents pursue a wide variety of objectives in a given environment, and the most relevant hypothesis is that by default, suitably strategic and intelligent agents, engaging in suitable types of planning, will have instrumental incentives to gain and maintain various types of power, since this power will help them pursue their objectives more effectively. Furthermore, as with any technology, AI systems could fail to behave in the way that their designers intend, and regardless of the intentions of the designers, the development and social impact of AI systems could fail, more broadly, to uphold and reflect important values. Thus, the main worry is that if we create and lose control of such agents, and their objectives are problematic, the result will be highly-capable, non-human agents actively working to gain and maintain power over their environment —agents in an adversarial relationship with humans who don’t want them to succeed.

This seems to be a key distinction between many risks humanity faces and this possible threat posed by power-seeking AI. The ability to cope in such a scenario could be close to 0 and, given this ability to cope is inversely related to risk —following a notional decomposition of risk such that Risk ≈ (Vulnerability x Exposure x Severity) / Ability to Cope —, it could be the case that building intelligent systems were a high-risk goal. The general backdrop picture is therefore illustrated by two main points: (a) Intelligent agency is an extremely powerful force for controlling and transforming the world, and (b) Building agents much more intelligent than humans is “playing with fire”. In the following, I will discuss the aforementioned hypothetical scenarios and I will estimate the probability of the six different hypotheses. Note that whenever I am discussing a given hypothesis, it implies that I am implicitly assuming all the previous ones.

[1]: It will become possible and financially feasible to build AI systems with three main properties: Advanced capabilities, Agentic planning, and Strategic awareness. (Call these “APS”—Advanced, Planning, Strategic—systems.)

I’m going to say: 75%. This milestone is the least discussed in the paper though, and therefore my estimate comes centrally from my own subjective forecast of the trajectory of AI progress —probably biased by intuitions.

Intelligence is the ability to learn to model a wide variety of domains and to construct plans that operate within and across those domains (Yudkowski). We tend to think of something like this when referring to human cognition (i.e. human cognition is one type of general intelligence). We divide this cognition as (a) learning+using a model of the world that represents causal relationships between actions/outputs/policies and outcomes to (b) select actions/outputs/policies that lead to outcomes that rate highly according to various criteria (e.g., objectives). There could exist an enormous amount of physically possible forms of cognitive systems which were much better at doing this than human cognition, and given the tremendous progress being made in the world of AI recently, it would not be surprising if in the next few years we were to come across the discovery of one of these kinds of intelligence.

Also, it is important to realize that human cognition —whatever kind of cognition with a biological embodiment— depends on a biological brain and therefore has some biological constraints such as cell count, energy, communication speed, signaling frequency, memory capacity, component reliability, input/output bandwidth… Therefore, if by any chance we were able to build something analogous to an APS system, there is an enormous chance that it could be much (MUCH MUCH) more intelligent than us. On the other side, it could be the case that general intelligence is an incredibly rare phenomenon (like >1M-σ OOD), and therefore it would be much harder to replicate it in a physical system. Indeed, it seems to be really strange biologically as all the rest of the intelligent beings we know are far less intelligent than us.

[2]: There will be strong incentives to build and deploy APS systems | (1).

I am going to say: 90%. The main reason for this estimate is its usefulness. Advanced capabilities are already useful in today's applications, and they are expected to be useful in the future. On the other hand, agentic planning can be a powerful way of interacting with an environment that does not afford a lot of opportunities for trial and error, and strategic awareness —“understand what is going on”— can be useful to interact with other agents and to recognize available routes to achieving goals. Thus, It seems that building APS systems could be tremendously useful to perform some tasks.

A great incentive would also be the existence of modularity within these systems. If we can divide the roadmap of a specific task into smaller parallelly computed subtasks, building APS systems would be more incentivized, as we could introduce humans into the process to supervise it, making it at least equally safe and probably safer. On the other side, the available techniques —it may be that creating APS systems is the only route, or the most efficient route, to automating a task, given available techniques— and byproducts of sophistication —these properties could arise in unexpected ways, and/or prove difficult to prevent— arguments also add some weight to consider the veracity of this hypothesis. Still, they are close to insignificant compared to usefulness.

The 10% on false, here, comes centrally from the possibility that the combination of agentic planning and strategic awareness isn’t that useful or necessary for any task. However, I tend to think that both planning and strategic awareness should be useful for something. It is prudent to think —as they are “extra” adjustments to advanced capabilities— that if only advanced capabilities were sufficient to perform X amount of tasks, APS systems could perform at least these same X amount of tasks (and probably some more). In any case, it is relevant to mention here that we need to be cautious in predicting what degree of agentic planning and/or strategic awareness will be necessary to perform some tasks. In particular, we should avoid reasonings like “Humans do X task using Y capabilities, so AI systems that aim to do X will also need Y capabilities” or “obviously task X needs Y capabilities”.

[3]: It will be hard to build APS systems that would not seek to gain and maintain power in unintended ways (problems with their objectives), but these systems will superficially be attractive to deploy anyway | (1)–(2).

I am going to say: 55%. Because of the difficulty in establishing accurate proxies, there is a considerably big chance that we build intelligent systems with misaligned behavior —unintended behavior that arises specifically in virtue of problems with objectives—. A key hypothesis here is that there is a close connection, in sufficiently advanced agents, between misaligned behavior in general, and misaligned power-seeking —active efforts by an AI system to gain and maintain power in ways that designers didn’t intend— in particular. This phenomenon is known as the Instrumental Convergence Thesis —if a system is less than fully-aligned, then expect that system to be less than fully-power-seeking-aligned— and has been proven as an empirical claim. In fact, even conceptually, this thesis makes a lot of sense. If a system detects that it is acting in a way that is inconsistent with the principles of its designers (but for the sole purpose of optimizing its objectives), it has strong incentives to acquire various forms of power with which to protect itself from counter-actions that the designers might take.

Also, it is important to realize what Bostrom (2014) identifies as “convergent instrumental goals,” each of which promotes an agent’s power to achieve its objectives. These include: self-preservation, “goal-content integrity,” (e.g. preventing changes to its objectives), improving its cognitive capability, technological development (control over more powerful technology tends to be useful), and resource-acquisition.

Here comes the challenge of power-seeking alignment, which can be broken down as follows: (1) Designers need to cause the APS system to be such that the objectives it pursues on some set of inputs X do not give rise to misaligned power-seeking, and (2) They (and other decision-makers) need to restrict the inputs the system receives to X. Thus, the most relevant subproblems to lower the probability of this hypothesis would be:

  • Controlling objectives. We constantly find problems with proxies —systems optimizing for imperfect proxies often behave in unintended ways—, problems with search —even if a criterion captures the intended behavior, systems may not end up intrinsically motivated by the criteria, e.g humans with passing on genes—, and myopia —focus on short-term objectives.
  • Controlling capabilities —note that less capable systems imply stronger incentives to cooperate with humans. This control can be achieved via specialization —how broad a system’s competencies— preventing problematic improvements —inputs a system receives result in improved capabilities—, and scaling —note that there exist strong incentives to scale up the capabilities of frontier systems.

There are also some extra difficulties to the challenge of power-seeking alignment, namely:

  • Barriers to understanding: safety and reliability usually require understanding a system well enough to predict its behavior. However, concerning current state-of-the-art neural networks, they turn out to be so complex that we currently cannot completely understand them. Therefore, it seems reasonable to think that this could be also the case with APS systems —as we expect the complexity of APS systems >> the complexity of current neural nets.
  • Adversarial dynamics. We would want to monitor/evaluate APS systems in order to detect problems with objectives. However, these systems could deceive trying to appear safer in order to manipulate our processes of understanding/judging them. An intelligent system could detect that manipulating the beliefs of its designers increases the chances of getting deployed and, therefore, optimizing its objectives.
  • Stakes of error. We need to understand the escalating impact of mistakes that a failure in building an APS system could entail —it could be similar to e.g. storing an engineered virus with a high chance of killing— and make us aware of the ridiculously large scale of negative value that a failure might bring.

This makes the overall difficulty/complexity/uncertainty so high. Concerning my estimate, the 45% on false is because (1) I think we could eventually solve some of the aforementioned subproblems, but (2) there is still huge uncertainty about this hypothesis (we need more empirical work to test it), and this makes me give a close-to-50 estimate.

[4]: Some deployed APS systems will be exposed to inputs where they seek power in unintended and high-impact ways | (1)–(3)

I am going to say: 85%. This hypothesis is also affected by the human-performed challenge of power-seeking alignment. In particular, we could control the circumstances of the APS systems to lower the probability of this event. This could be done by controlling options —what a circumstance makes it possible for a system to do, e.g. an APS system without internet access cannot self-replicate into more computers through the internet— or by controlling incentives —controlling which options make sense to choose, given some set of objectives. However, there could be so huge amounts of inputs we would want to hide and the problem of classifying which ones of them would be malicious could be highly complex. This makes the control of circumstances so difficult, and for me, it is reasonable to think that if a misaligned-power-seeking AI is deployed it would probably end up encountering some malicious input.

It is important to notice here that just because a technology is hard to make safe, it doesn't necessarily follow that many people will use it dangerously. Instead, they might modify their usage to take into account the level of safety attained. Thus, if we were unable to create reliable crash-free aircraft, we wouldn't anticipate a constant stream of fatal crashes, but rather a decline in air travel. Something similar could happen with APS systems. If we detect they could be dangerous if exposed to some given inputs, we would decrease their usage —therefore, their exposure to malicious inputs.

A relevant factor here is the timing of problems, as before-deployment problems are a priori preferable to after-deployment failures. However, not all pre-deployment problems will be detected —e.g. there could exist APS systems deceiving in order to get deployed—, and also pre-deployment well-behaved APS systems could misbehave after deployment as this would expose them to new inputs, opportunities, and capabilities. Another relevant issue is the factors influencing decisions about deployment which will be mainly two: (1) decision-makers' beliefs, and (2) cost-benefits relevantly perceived. Note that both of them could be modified by the deceiving behavior of APS systems.

The key risk factors for a hasty deployment are externalities and competition —since there are “first-mover advantages”, this could incentivize shortcuts on safety procedures—, the number of relevant actors —a larger number of actors implies bigger competitive dynamics, and this could modify beliefs, values, and incentives that inform the decision-making—, and bottlenecks on usefulness —if they turn out to be not useful, we could expect not intentional deployment. Regarding this last key factor, here comes again the idea of deception. AI systems could understand the behavior humans look for during training/testing and act this way to make us think they are more useful than they actually are —and behave distinctly after deployment.

[5]: Some of this power-seeking will scale (in aggregate) to the point of permanently disempowering ~all of humanity | (1)–(4).

I am going to say 20%. Discussions of existential risk from misaligned AI often focus on “take-off” scenarios —the transition from some lower level of frontier AI capability A to some much higher and riskier level B. When it comes to preventing these types of events, we have some tools that can help us to prevent them. One of them is warning shots which consist of experimenting with weaker systems in controlled environments to trigger tendencies towards misaligned power-seeking. However, relying on this tends to be over-optimistic for several reasons: (a) recognizing a problem does not solve it, (b) there will be fewer warning shots as capabilities increase, and (c) even with inadequate techniques, some actors could still push forward with deployment.

Competition for power among power-seeking agents (including humans) will also be a factor to consider. However, both unipolar and multipolar scenarios governed by AI systems won’t matter that much regarding risk. What matters is whether humans are disempowered or not. There would be a struggle between power-seeking agents' capabilities and the strength of the human opposition —employing e.g. corrective feedback loops— and therefore it would be relevant to know where APS systems' abilities would be based in the range [almost equal or slightly better than humans <--> arbitrarily capable].

My estimate for this hypothesis is mainly supported by the idea of power. There are two main types of power: hard power —the use of force to influence the behavior or interests of other actors— and soft power —the use of cultural tools to persuade other actors to do something. It might be relatively difficult for these APS systems to get hold of an embodiment with which to exercise a hard-power type. Regarding soft power, I consider that persuasion has its limits, and convincing an agent (with human intelligence level) to do something that blatantly goes against its interests could be difficult to achieve, even for an APS system.

[6]: This disempowerment will constitute an existential catastrophe | (1)–(5).

I am going to say: 90%. The term catastrophe here is understood as an event that drastically reduces the value of the trajectories along which human civilization could realistically develop (Ord, 2020). If (1)–(5) happens, that would mean the disempowerment of humanity. This means that humans would take a back seat and the most relevant decisions taken on the planet would be made without considering us.

The point to consider why disempowerment could not be a catastrophe comes with the “intrinsic rightness” argument. It argues that cognitive systems will converge on similar objectives within the limits of intelligence. However, it seems that “intrinsic rightness” is a bad reason for expecting convergence. The object that differentiates us as moral agents is the fact that —apart from being intelligent— we can feel certain kinds of negative (fear, anguish) and positive (joy, enthusiasm) experiences. If these systems do not possess these qualities, we should not expect for them any kind of moral standard, and this would make it difficult to converge with human values.

My estimate here is mainly motivated by the paradigm shift that this situation would entail. A good proxy to understand what this new scenario could look like is by looking at the relationship humans have with other animals and observing how we “keep their fates in our hands” (factory farms, fashion, scientific advancement, exotic pets…). This occurs mainly because (1) we are more intelligent, and (2) we act upon our interests and if they conflict with other animals' interests, there is a strong incentive to pass these interests over. Observe that something similar to this could happen with the disempowerment of humanity.

According to my estimates, multiplying these conditional probabilities together, then, we get 75% · 90% · 55% · 85% · 20% · 90% = ~4% probability of existential catastrophe from misaligned, power-seeking AI by 2070. However, as Carlsmith comments in the paper, the main point here isn’t the specific numbers. Rather, it is that there could be a disturbingly substantive risk that we (or our children) live to see humanity as a whole permanently and involuntarily disempowered by AI systems we’ve lost control over. What we can and should do about this now is a further question. But the issue seems serious.
