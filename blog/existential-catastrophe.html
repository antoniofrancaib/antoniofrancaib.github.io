Power-Seeking AI and Existential Risk

Some people are concerned that the advancement of Artificial Intelligence may lead to an existential catastrophe, that is, the destruction of humanity’s long-term potential. The following is a brief distillation of Open Philanthropy Senior Research Analyst Joseph Carlsmith’s "Is power-seeking AI an existential risk?" paper, where a concrete AI-related catastrophic scenario is depicted. This scenario includes a given set of hypothetical events and the final aim is to assign a probability of the occurrence of these hypotheses by 2070—just to mean within our lifetimes.

The conceptual use of “power” here means something like the type of thing that helps a wide variety of agents pursue a wide variety of objectives in a given environment, and the most relevant hypothesis is that by default, suitably strategic and intelligent agents, engaging in suitable types of planning, will have instrumental incentives to gain and maintain various types of power, since this power will help them pursue their objectives more effectively. Furthermore, as with any technology, AI systems could fail to behave in the way that their designers intend, and regardless of the intentions of the designers, the development and social impact of AI systems could fail, more broadly, to uphold and reflect important values. Thus, the main worry is that if we create and lose control of such agents, and their objectives are problematic, the result will be highly-capable, non-human agents actively working to gain and maintain power over their environment —agents in an adversarial relationship with humans who don’t want them to succeed.

This seems to be a key distinction between many risks humanity faces and this possible threat posed by power-seeking AI. The ability to cope in such a scenario could be close to 0 and, given this ability to cope is inversely related to risk —following a notional decomposition of risk such that Risk ≈ (Vulnerability x Exposure x Severity) / Ability to Cope —, it could be the case that building intelligent systems were a high-risk goal. The general backdrop picture is therefore illustrated by two main points: (a) Intelligent agency is an extremely powerful force for controlling and transforming the world, and (b) Building agents much more intelligent than humans is “playing with fire”. In the following, I will discuss the aforementioned hypothetical scenarios and I will estimate the probability of the six different hypotheses. Note that whenever I am discussing a given hypothesis, it implies that I am implicitly assuming all the previous ones.

[1]: It will become possible and financially feasible to build AI systems with three main properties: Advanced capabilities, Agentic planning, and Strategic awareness. (Call these “APS”—Advanced, Planning, Strategic—systems.)

I’m going to say: 75%. This milestone is the least discussed in the paper though, and therefore my estimate comes centrally from my own subjective forecast of the trajectory of AI progress —probably biased by intuitions.

Intelligence is the ability to learn to model a wide variety of domains and to construct plans that operate within and across those domains (Yudkowski). We tend to think of something like this when referring to human cognition (i.e. human cognition is one type of general intelligence). We divide this cognition as (a) learning+using a model of the world that represents causal relationships between actions/outputs/policies and outcomes to (b) select actions/outputs/policies that lead to outcomes that rate highly according to various criteria (e.g., objectives). There could exist an enormous amount of physically possible forms of cognitive systems which were much better at doing this than human cognition, and given the tremendous progress being made in the world of AI recently, it would not be surprising if in the next few years we were to come across the discovery of one of these kinds of intelligence.

Also, it is important to realize that human cognition —whatever kind of cognition with a biological embodiment— depends on a biological brain and therefore has some biological constraints such as cell count, energy, communication speed, signaling frequency, memory capacity, component reliability, input/output bandwidth… Therefore, if by any chance we were able to build something analogous to an APS system, there is an enormous chance that it could be much (MUCH MUCH) more intelligent than us. On the other side, it could be the case that general intelligence is an incredibly rare phenomenon (like >1M-σ OOD), and therefore it would be much harder to replicate it in a physical system. Indeed, it seems to be really strange biologically as all the rest of the intelligent beings we know are far less intelligent than us.

[2]: There will be strong incentives to build and deploy APS systems | (1).

I am going to say: 90%. The main reason for this estimate is its usefulness. Advanced capabilities are already useful in today's applications, and they are expected to be useful in the future. On the other hand, agentic planning can be a powerful way of interacting with an environment that does not afford a lot of opportunities for trial and error, and strategic awareness —“understand what is going on”— can be useful to interact with other agents and to recognize available routes to achieving goals. Thus, It seems that building APS systems could be tremendously useful to perform some tasks.

A great incentive would also be the existence of modularity within these systems. If we can divide the roadmap of a specific task into smaller parallelly computed subtasks, building APS systems would be more incentivized, as we could introduce humans into the process to supervise it, making it at least equally safe and probably safer. On the other side, the available techniques —it may be that creating APS systems is the only route, or the most efficient route, to automating a task, given available techniques— and byproducts of sophistication —these properties could arise in unexpected ways, and/or prove difficult to prevent— arguments also add some weight to consider the veracity of this hypothesis. Still, they are close to insignificant compared to usefulness.

The 10% on false, here, comes centrally from the possibility that the combination of agentic planning and strategic awareness isn’t that useful or necessary for any task. However, I tend to think that both planning and strategic awareness should be useful for something. It is prudent to think —as they are “extra” adjustments to advanced capabilities— that if only advanced capabilities were sufficient to perform X amount of tasks, APS systems could perform at least these same X amount of tasks (and probably some more). In any case, it is relevant to mention here that we need to be cautious in predicting what degree of agentic planning and/or strategic awareness will be necessary to perform some tasks. In particular, we should avoid reasonings like “Humans do X task using Y capabilities, so AI systems that aim to do X will also need Y capabilities” or “obviously task X needs Y capabilities”.

[3]: It will be hard to build APS systems that would not seek to gain and maintain power in unintended ways (problems with their objectives), but these systems will superficially be attractive to deploy anyway | (1)–(2).

I am going to say: 55%. Because of the difficulty in establishing accurate proxies, there is a considerably big chance that we build intelligent systems with misaligned behavior —unintended behavior that arises specifically in virtue of problems with objectives—. A key hypothesis here is that there is a close connection, in sufficiently advanced agents, between misaligned behavior in general, and misaligned power-seeking —active efforts by an AI system to gain and maintain power in ways that designers didn’t intend— in particular. This phenomenon is known as the Instrumental Convergence Thesis —if a system is less than fully-aligned, then expect that system to be less than fully-power-seeking-aligned— and has been proven as an empirical claim. In fact, even conceptually, this thesis makes a lot of sense. If a system detects that it is acting in a way that is inconsistent with the principles of its designers (but for the sole purpose of optimizing its objectives), it has strong incentives to acquire various forms of power with which to protect itself from counter-actions that the designers might take.

Also, it is important to realize what Bostrom (2014) identifies as “convergent instrumental goals,” each of which promotes an agent’s power to achieve its objectives. These include: self-preservation, “goal-content integrity,” (e.g. preventing changes to its objectives), improving its cognitive capability, technological development (control over more powerful technology tends to be useful), and resource-acquisition.

Here comes the challenge of power-seeking alignment, which can be broken down as follows: (1) Designers need to cause the APS system to be such that the objectives it pursues on some set of inputs X do not give rise to misaligned power-seeking, and (2) They (and other decision-makers) need to restrict the inputs the system receives to X.
